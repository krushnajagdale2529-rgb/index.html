📖 About the Great Wall of China

The Great Wall of China is one of the most iconic and awe-inspiring structures ever created by humankind. It is a massive series of fortifications made from stone, brick, tamped earth, wood, and various other materials, stretching across the northern borders of China. The wall was primarily constructed to defend Chinese states and empires from the invasions and raids of nomadic tribes from the north.

The wall stretches approximately 21,196 kilometres (13,171 miles) in total length, though the most well-preserved and frequently visited sections were built during the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644 AD). It runs from Shanhaiguan in the east to Jiayuguan in the west, winding through rugged mountains, vast deserts, and fertile plains. The wall typically stands between 5 to 8 metres tall and is wide enough for five soldiers to march side by side.

Construction of the wall began as early as the 7th century BC, with various Chinese warring states building separate walls for their own protection. It was the first Emperor of unified China, Qin Shi Huang, who around 221 BC ordered these separate walls to be connected and extended, giving birth to the first unified Great Wall. Successive dynasties — Han, Sui, and Ming — further repaired, rebuilt, and extended the wall over the centuries.

The Great Wall holds enormous historical, cultural, and symbolic importance not just for China, but for the entire world. It stands as a testament to the determination, ingenuity, and sacrificial labour of millions of Chinese workers. In 1987, it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and it continues to attract millions of tourists every year who come to marvel at its grandeur and timeless significance.

📅 Historical Timeline

7th Century BC
Individual Chinese states begin constructing separate defensive walls to protect their territories from northern nomads.
221 BC
Emperor Qin Shi Huang unifies China and orders the walls to be connected, forming the first Great Wall.
206 BC – 220 AD
The Han Dynasty extends the wall westward into Central Asia to protect the famous Silk Road trade routes.
1368 – 1644 AD
The Ming Dynasty undertakes the most extensive construction, building the well-known brick-and-stone wall seen today.
1987
UNESCO designates the Great Wall as a World Heritage Site, recognising its outstanding universal value to humanity.
2007
The Great Wall is declared one of the New Seven Wonders of the World by a global public vote of over 100 million people.

💡 Interesting Facts

  • The Great Wall is often called the "longest cemetery on Earth" — over one million workers are believed to have died during its construction and were buried within the wall itself.
  • Contrary to popular belief, the Great Wall is not visible from space with the naked eye — this long-standing myth has been officially debunked by astronauts including China's own Yang Liwei.
  • During the Ming Dynasty, sticky rice was mixed into the mortar between bricks, making the wall exceptionally durable — some sections remain solid to this very day because of it.
  • The wall was built by a workforce of soldiers, peasants, and criminals who were forced into gruelling labour under the orders of ruling emperors.
  • Today, nearly 30% of the original wall has disappeared due to erosion, vandalism, and locals using its bricks for building their own homes over the centuries.
  • The Great Wall does not run in a single straight line — it branches, loops, and has multiple parallel sections in some regions, making its exact total length difficult to measure precisely.
